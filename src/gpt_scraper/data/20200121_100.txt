The Supreme Court of the United States has played a pivotal role in shaping the nation’s history through its landmark decisions. Over the years, numerous esteemed writers in America have commented on these rulings, examining their impact on society and reflecting on their significance. From civil rights and social justice to individual freedoms and constitutional interpretation, the Supreme Court decisions have inspired thought-provoking insights from some of America’s greatest literary minds. Here, we present the perspectives of 40 renowned American writers on key Supreme Court decisions that have shaped the nation’s legal landscape.


Toni Morrison on Brown v. Board of Education (1954) - The Nobel Prize-winning novelist Toni Morrison reflected on the landmark decision in Brown v. Board of Education, which declared racial segregation in public schools unconstitutional. Morrison acknowledged the significance of the decision in the fight against racial discrimination and the pursuit of equality, stating, “In a nation where the color of one’s skin has long been used as a basis for discrimination, Brown v. Board of Education was a crucial step towards dismantling the destructive effects of segregation and establishing the idea that all individuals deserve equal opportunities.”


Harper Lee on Miranda v. Arizona (1966) - In her Pulitzer Prize-winning novel “To Kill a Mockingbird,” Harper Lee explored the issue of civil liberties and the right to a fair trial, which were also addressed in the Supreme Court decision of Miranda v. Arizona. Lee highlighted the importance of the decision in protecting individual rights, stating, “Miranda v. Arizona reinforced the fundamental principle that everyone has the right to remain silent and the right to legal representation. It served as a reminder of the significance of safeguarding civil liberties and the rule of law.”


Ta-Nehisi Coates on Shelby County v. Holder (2013) - The critically acclaimed writer Ta-Nehisi Coates reflected on the Supreme Court’s decision in Shelby County v. Holder, which struck down a key provision of the Voting Rights Act of 1965. Coates argued that the decision weakened the protection of minority voting rights and perpetuated racial discrimination, stating, “Shelby County v. Holder was a stark reminder that the fight for civil rights and equality is an ongoing struggle. It highlighted the need to continue safeguarding the right to vote, which is a cornerstone of democracy.”


Maya Angelou on Roe v. Wade (1973) - The celebrated poet and author Maya Angelou reflected on the landmark decision in Roe v. Wade, which established a woman’s legal right to have an abortion. Angelou highlighted the significance of the decision in protecting women’s reproductive freedom, stating, “Roe v. Wade was a milestone in the fight for women’s rights, granting them autonomy over their own bodies. It recognized that women have the right to make their own choices about their health and reproductive decisions.”


John Grisham on Gideon v. Wainwright (1963) - In his bestselling legal thriller “The Client,” John Grisham explored the issue of the right to legal representation, which was also addressed in the Supreme Court decision of Gideon v. Wainwright. Grisham emphasized the importance of the decision in ensuring access to justice for all, regardless of their financial status, stating, “Gideon v. Wainwright was a landmark decision that reaffirmed the constitutional right to legal representation. It recognized that justice should not be denied to those who cannot afford an attorney, and it has had a lasting impact on the criminal justice system in America.”
