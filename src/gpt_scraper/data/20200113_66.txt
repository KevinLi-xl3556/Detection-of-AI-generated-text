Nelson Bryant, a renowned writer and journalist known as the “Supreme Chronicler” of outdoor life, has passed away at the age of 96. His death marks the end of a long and illustrious career that spanned over six decades and left an indelible mark on the world of outdoor writing.
Bryant was born in 1927 in the small town of Tenafly, New Jersey. His love for the outdoors began at a young age, and he spent much of his childhood exploring the woods and streams near his home. After serving in the United States Navy during World War II, Bryant attended Yale University, where he earned a degree in English.
Bryant began his career as a journalist in the 1950s, writing for the New York Herald Tribune and later the New York Times. He quickly established himself as a gifted storyteller and an authority on outdoor topics such as hunting, fishing, and conservation. Over the years, he covered a wide range of subjects, from the struggles of the endangered whooping crane to the joys of fly-fishing in the Rocky Mountains.

His writing inspired countless readers to explore the natural world and to appreciate its beauty and complexity.
Bryant’s contributions to outdoor writing were recognized with numerous awards and honors throughout his career. In 1995, he was inducted into the Outdoor Writers Association of America’s Hall of Fame, and in 2007 he received the Lifetime Achievement Award from the New England Outdoor Writers Association.
